04.2 / Internal Components
LED Bulb Cutaway

Inside every LED bulb is a miniature switching power supply. This cutaway reveals the components responsible for generating electromagnetic interference -- and why cheaper bulbs produce dramatically more noise.

Interactive 3D — Drag to rotate

LED Array

Surface-mount LEDs (typically 2835 or 5730 packages) soldered to an aluminum PCB. The LEDs themselves produce no EMI -- they're simple diodes. The light output is proportional to current, not voltage, which is why a constant-current driver is needed.

Switching Driver PCB

The EMI culprit. A tiny circuit board containing a switching MOSFET, inductor, and control IC that converts 120V AC to ~30V DC at high frequency (typically 40-200 kHz). The rapid on/off switching of the MOSFET through the inductor generates broadband harmonics extending well into the hundreds of MHz.

Heat Sink

Aluminum fins dissipate heat from both the LEDs and the driver circuit. In cheap bulbs, the heat sink is undersized, causing the driver to run hotter and switch less cleanly -- producing even more EMI. Quality bulbs use larger heat sinks with proper thermal paste contact.

EMI Filtering (or lack thereof)

Compliant bulbs include input filtering: X/Y safety capacitors, common-mode chokes, and sometimes a small metal shield over the driver PCB. Cheap imports skip these components to save $0.15-0.30 per bulb. The result: 10-100x more conducted and radiated emissions.

Technician Tip

The single most common cause of reduced remote range (after LED interference) is a damaged or improperly routed antenna wire. The wire should hang straight down from the opener housing, fully extended to its original length (~9.5"). Never cut, coil, or tuck the antenna wire. If the wire is missing, a 9.5" piece of 20-gauge insulated wire works as a replacement.

04.5 / Regulatory
FCC Part 15 & LED Compliance

FCC Part 15 sets limits for unintentional electromagnetic emissions from electronic devices. LED bulbs are classified as "unintentional radiators" -- they're not designed to transmit RF, but their switching circuits do. Here's why compliance matters.

FCC-Compliant LED Bulb

  • Has FCC ID or Declaration of Conformity
  • Includes EMI input filter (X/Y caps, choke)
  • Shielded driver PCB with ground plane
  • Emissions below Part 15.109 limits
  • Tested at accredited lab (per ANSI C63.4)
  • Typical price: $4-8 per bulb
  • Examples: Genie, Chamberlain, Philips brands

Non-Compliant LED Bulb

  • No FCC markings or fake FCC ID
  • No input filter -- bare minimum components
  • Unshielded driver, no ground plane
  • Emissions 10-100x above Part 15 limits
  • No lab testing documentation
  • Typical price: $1-3 per bulb (multi-packs)
  • Common on Amazon, Temu, bulk import sites

Part 15.109 Limits

For Class B (residential) unintentional radiators, FCC limits radiated emissions to ~40 dBuV/m at 300 MHz (measured at 3 meters). Cheap LED bulbs can exceed this by 20-40 dB -- that's 10x to 100x more power than allowed. The FCC rarely enforces against individual bulbs, which is why the market is flooded with non-compliant products.

How to Check Compliance

Look for the FCC logo or text "This device complies with Part 15 of the FCC Rules" on the packaging. You can verify an FCC ID at fcc.gov/oet/ea/fccid. However, many compliant bulbs use a "Supplier's Declaration of Conformity" (SDoC) which doesn't have a searchable ID. Brand-name bulbs from major manufacturers are generally safe.

Garage Door-Rated Bulbs

Several manufacturers now sell bulbs specifically marketed for garage door openers: Genie LED (A19, 800 lumen), Chamberlain CHLED, and various "radio-safe" branded options. These include extra EMI filtering and are tested to not interfere with 300-400 MHz receivers. They cost $3-5 more than generic bulbs but eliminate interference problems entirely.

DIY Fix: Ferrite Chokes

Snap-on ferrite chokes (cores) placed on the wiring close to the bulb socket can reduce conducted EMI by 10-20 dB. They work by converting high-frequency noise into heat. Use a mix-31 or mix-43 ferrite core rated for 200-500 MHz. This is a $2 fix that can improve marginal interference cases but won't solve severe problems from very cheap bulbs.

Why FCC Enforcement Is Weak

The FCC's Enforcement Bureau prioritizes intentional radiators (illegal radio transmitters, jamming devices) over unintentional radiators like LED bulbs. With millions of non-compliant bulbs imported annually, enforcement is practically impossible at the product level. The burden falls on consumers to choose compliant products -- which is why education (like this section) matters.

05.1 / Security
Rolling Code Encryption

Rolling code (also called "hopping code") prevents replay attacks by generating a new encrypted code with every button press. The remote and opener share a synchronized counter and encryption key -- if you capture and replay a code, it's already expired.

How Security+ 1.0 Works

The original Security+ protocol (2005) uses a combination of a fixed code portion (identifying the specific remote) and a rolling code portion (a counter encrypted with a shared key). Each transmission increments the counter by 1-3 steps. The opener accepts codes within a "window" of the next ~256 expected values, allowing for out-of-range button presses.

Vulnerability: Known Plaintext

Security+ 1.0's fixed portion means an attacker who captures enough transmissions can potentially derive the fixed code. The rolling portion uses a relatively simple XOR-based cipher. While replay attacks fail (the code is already used), sophisticated attacks combining jamming + capture can work against 1.0 -- the attacker jams the real signal while capturing the code for later use.

How Security+ 2.0 Works

Security+ 2.0 (2011+) eliminates the fixed code entirely. The entire transmission is encrypted using AES-like block cipher with a 32-bit rolling counter. The remote and opener share a secret key established during programming. Each code is unique and reveals nothing about the key or the next code. Even capturing millions of transmissions provides no mathematical advantage for predicting future codes.

Why Replay Attacks Fail

The opener maintains a "window" of acceptable counter values (typically next 256 codes). Once a code is received and validated, the window advances past it. A replayed code falls behind the window and is rejected. The jam-and-capture attack from 1.0 is also defeated because 2.0 uses a challenge-response element during the resynchronization process.

Cloud-Dependent (myQ)

Phone → myQ Cloud (AWS) → Home Router → Opener Wi-Fi Module. Every command traverses the internet, even when you're standing in the garage. If myQ servers go down (which happens), no remote control. Chamberlain has also started charging subscription fees and restricting third-party API access. Typical latency: 2-5 seconds.

Local Control (Z-Wave/Zigbee)

Phone → Local Hub (SmartThings/Hubitat) → Z-Wave/Zigbee → Controller → Opener. Commands stay on your local network. No internet dependency, no cloud outages, no subscription fees. Latency: <500ms. Requires a dedicated smart home hub. Z-Wave devices like GoControl and Zooz relay wire to the opener's button terminals.

Matter over Thread

Phone → Thread Border Router → Thread Mesh → Opener. Matter provides local control with optional cloud connectivity for remote access. Thread's mesh networking means no Wi-Fi dependency. Universal compatibility: HomeKit, Google Home, Alexa, SmartThings -- all with one protocol. Low power, low latency (<300ms local).

05.4 / Open Source
Ratgdo & Local Control

The ratgdo (Rage Against the Garage Door Opener) project is an open-source ESP8266/ESP32-based board that taps directly into the Security+ 2.0 serial bus on Chamberlain/LiftMaster openers. It provides full local control via MQTT or ESPHome -- no cloud, no subscriptions, no API restrictions.

Ratgdo wiring & communication flow

How It Works

Ratgdo connects to the opener's serial bus (the same bus used by wall consoles and wireless keypads). It speaks the Security+ 2.0 protocol natively, sending open/close/light commands and reading door state, obstruction status, and light state. Unlike relay-based retrofits, ratgdo provides true status feedback -- it knows if the door is open, closed, opening, or closing.

MQTT / ESPHome Integration

The board runs either MQTT firmware (for any MQTT broker like Mosquitto) or ESPHome firmware (native Home Assistant integration). ESPHome provides auto-discovery in Home Assistant -- the device shows up automatically with open/close controls, door state sensor, light toggle, and obstruction sensor. No cloud dependency, no polling, instant state updates via the serial bus.

Wiring

Three-wire connection to the opener: Red (3.3V power from opener logic board), White (serial data), Black/Shield (ground). The serial bus runs at 9600 baud with an inverted signal. Ratgdo includes an onboard voltage regulator and level shifter. Compatible with Security+ 2.0 openers manufactured 2011 and later.

Why It Matters

Chamberlain's decision to block third-party myQ API access (2023) left many smart home users without garage door control in Home Assistant, HomeKit (without the $70 bridge), and other platforms. Ratgdo restores and exceeds that functionality with faster response times, no internet dependency, and no risk of future API lockouts. It's the community's answer to vendor lock-in.

Where to Get Ratgdo

The ratgdo project is open-source hardware. Pre-assembled boards are available from ratgdo.com and community group buys. The ESP8266 version is ~$30, the newer ESP32 version ~$35. Both include all necessary components and a 3D-printable case. Firmware updates are done over Wi-Fi (OTA). Full documentation, wiring diagrams, and firmware at github.com/PaulWieland/ratgdo.

05.5 / Future
Matter Protocol & the Future

Matter is the smart home industry's attempt to end the protocol wars. Backed by Apple, Google, Amazon, and Samsung, Matter over Thread promises a future where any garage door opener works with any smart home platform -- locally, securely, and without a cloud subscription.

What Is Matter?

Matter is an application-layer protocol that runs over Wi-Fi, Thread, or Ethernet. It defines standard device types (including "garage door controller") with standardized commands (open, close, status). Any Matter-certified device works with any Matter-certified controller -- HomeKit, Google Home, Alexa, SmartThings -- out of the box, with no custom integrations needed.

Thread vs Wi-Fi

Thread is a low-power mesh networking protocol (based on IEEE 802.15.4) designed for IoT. Unlike Wi-Fi, Thread devices form a self-healing mesh -- each device extends the network's range. Thread Border Routers (built into HomePod Mini, Nest Hub, Echo Gen 4) bridge Thread to your IP network. For garage door openers, Thread means reliable connectivity without Wi-Fi range issues in the garage.

Local-First Control

Matter's key principle: local control is mandatory, cloud is optional. Every Matter command must work without internet connectivity. Cloud access (for remote control away from home) is an optional add-on, not a requirement. This eliminates the single point of failure that plagues myQ and similar cloud-dependent systems.

Security Model

Matter uses CASE (Certificate Authenticated Session Establishment) with per-device certificates, AES-128-CCM encryption, and secure commissioning via QR code or NFC. Each controller (your phone, hub) gets its own "fabric" credential. Multi-admin is built in -- you can have HomeKit AND Google Home controlling the same device without conflict.

Matter Garage Door Timeline

Matter 1.0 released -- Initial device types include lights, switches, sensors. Garage door controller type defined but no certified products yet.
2023
Matter 1.2 -- Adds explicit "Barrier/Garage Door" device type with open/close/stop commands and safety features (obstruction detection reporting). Chamberlain announces myQ API lockdown, driving demand for alternatives.
2024
First Matter garage door controllers appear -- Primarily retrofit devices (similar to ratgdo concept) with Matter over Wi-Fi. ESPHome adds Matter support, enabling DIY Matter garage door controllers on ESP32.
2025
Thread-based garage door products -- Meross and other manufacturers release Thread-enabled controllers. Native opener integration (not relay-based) begins appearing from smaller manufacturers.
2026+
Native Matter in openers -- Major manufacturers expected to include Matter/Thread radios directly in new openers, eliminating the need for retrofit devices entirely. The endgame: buy any opener, it works with any platform, locally, out of the box.

What This Means for Homeowners

If you're buying a new opener today, check for Matter compatibility -- it future-proofs your investment. If you have an existing opener, a Matter-over-Wi-Fi or Matter-over-Thread retrofit device will provide universal smart home integration without locking you into any single ecosystem. The days of "works with Alexa but not HomeKit" are ending.
