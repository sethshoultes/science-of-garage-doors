IPPT Formula

Inch-Pounds Per Turn: IPPT = d4G / (10.8DN) where d = wire diameter, G = shear modulus (11.5x106 psi for oil-tempered wire), D = mean coil diameter (ID + d), N = active coils. This is the fundamental spring specification for matching springs to doors.

Wahl Correction Factor

Wire stress is not uniform across the cross-section. The Wahl factor K = (4C - 1)/(4C - 4) + 0.615/C corrects for curvature effects, where C = D/d is the spring index. Lower C (fatter wire relative to coil) means higher stress concentration on the inner coil surface.

Cycle Life (Goodman)

Cycle life is estimated from the Goodman fatigue diagram for ASTM A229 wire. The ratio of corrected stress to ultimate tensile strength determines the endurance limit. Standard springs at 45-50% UTS yield ~10,000 cycles. High-cycle springs use superior wire grades to lower this ratio.

Dual Spring Sizing

For dual-spring systems, each spring provides half the required IPPT. The calculator shows single-spring values — for a dual system, double the "Door Weight Range" shown. Always use two springs for doors over 200 lbs for redundancy and balanced force distribution.

Engineering Insight — The Balance Zone

A perfectly balanced door has spring force matching door weight within 5 lbs at every position. In practice, the spring force curve (linear with angle) and the door weight curve (nonlinear due to geometry) never match perfectly. The "dead spots" where they diverge most are at the very bottom (door feels heavy) and mid-curve (door can feel light). Quality installation minimizes the maximum imbalance to under 8 lbs across the full travel.

Spring Force Curve

Torsion spring force is linear with angular displacement: F = (IPPT x turns x 2) / drum_diameter. As the door opens, the spring unwinds and force decreases linearly. The cable drum's variable radius partially compensates, but the overall curve slopes downward from closed to open.

Door Weight Curve

A sectional door's effective weight is NOT constant. At 0% open (vertical), gravity pulls straight down and cables bear full weight. Through the radius curve (20-40% travel), weight redistributes as panels transition. When fully horizontal, the center of mass shifts and effective weight at the cable attachment point changes based on panel count and door height.

Worm Gear Reduction

The worm (helical screw on motor shaft) meshes with the worm wheel (large gear). For a single-start worm, the ratio equals the number of teeth on the wheel. A 40:1 ratio means 40 motor revolutions produce 1 output revolution, but torque is multiplied by 40 (minus friction losses).

Self-Locking Property

When the worm gear's lead angle is less than the friction angle (typically below ~6 degrees), the gear set is "self-locking" — the output shaft cannot drive the worm backwards. This means the door's weight cannot back-drive the motor, acting as a built-in brake. Most garage door worm gears operate at lead angles of 3-5 degrees, ensuring reliable self-locking.

Efficiency vs Ratio

Worm gear efficiency is typically 30-50% due to sliding contact between the worm threads and wheel teeth. Higher ratios generally mean lower efficiency because of the steeper helix angle mismatch. The "lost" energy becomes heat, which is why opener motors are warm after extended operation. Synthetic lubricants can improve efficiency by 5-10%.

Why Not Spur Gears?

Spur gears are more efficient (~95%) but don't self-lock. Without self-locking, you'd need a separate brake mechanism to hold the door at any position when the motor stops. Worm gears provide compact, high-ratio reduction AND built-in braking in a single stage — the perfect combination for garage door openers.

Spring Failure Warning Signs

As springs degrade, the door becomes increasingly difficult to lift manually and the opener motor works harder (draws more current). If your door feels significantly heavier than when installed, or if the opener struggles and strains, the springs are nearing end-of-life. Replace springs proactively — a broken spring under tension releases stored energy catastrophically.

Degradation Curve

Spring force doesn't drop linearly. The first 70% of cycle life shows minimal degradation (~5% force loss). Between 70-90% of rated life, degradation accelerates as micro-cracks propagate in the wire. The final 10% shows rapid force loss and imminent failure risk. This is the S-curve shown in the chart above.

Temperature Effects

Extreme temperatures affect spring life. Cold winters (<20F) increase wire brittleness and reduce cycle life by up to 20%. Hot climates (>100F) accelerate fatigue through thermal stress cycling. Garages with extreme temperature swings see the worst spring longevity. The temperature factor slider models this effect.

Usage Patterns

Average residential use is 3-5 cycles/day (each open+close = 1 cycle). Families with teenagers or home businesses may see 8-10 cycles/day. A standard 10,000-cycle spring at 4 cycles/day lasts ~6.8 years. Doubling usage to 8 cycles/day cuts life to ~3.4 years. High-cycle springs are cost-effective for heavy use.

Maintenance Impact

Regular lubrication with silicone-based spray reduces friction-induced stress and can extend spring life by 15-20%. Annual inspection and lubrication is the single best maintenance action for garage door longevity. Never use WD-40 (it strips existing lubricant) — use a dedicated garage door lubricant or white lithium grease.

UL 325 — Federal Entrapment Protection Standard

Since 1993, all residential garage door openers must comply with UL 325, which mandates automatic reversal when the closing door contacts an obstruction with more than 15 lbf (pounds-force). The opener must also reverse within 2 seconds of contact. Two independent safety systems are required: (1) mechanical force sensing via motor current monitoring, and (2) photoelectric sensors (IR beam at 6" height). Both must function for the opener to operate in close mode.

Force Calculation

The force at the bottom edge combines: (1) unbalanced door weight (total weight minus spring counterbalance), (2) motor driving force, and (3) momentum from closing speed. F = F_motor + (W - F_spring) + (m x v / t_stop). Proper spring balance is critical — an unbalanced door adds its weight deficit directly to the closing force.

The 15 lbf Threshold

UL 325 specifies a maximum of 15 lbf applied over no more than 2 inches of travel after contact. This equates to roughly 15 pound-force — about the weight of a gallon of milk. The opener's force adjustment controls how much motor current is needed before the controller detects an obstruction and triggers reversal. Setting force too high defeats this safety mechanism.

Spring Balance Matters

An unbalanced door is a safety hazard. If the springs provide less counterbalance than the door weighs, the motor must push harder to close, increasing the force at the bottom edge. A door that's 20 lbs out of balance adds that full 20 lbs to the closing force. This is why spring adjustment should precede force adjustment during installation.

Testing Compliance

To test: place a 2x4 flat on the floor in the door's path. The door should reverse upon contacting the board (1.5" obstruction). If it doesn't reverse, reduce the close force setting. If it reverses before touching, increase slightly. The door should lift a minimum of 2 inches after contact before stopping or reversing fully. Test monthly.
