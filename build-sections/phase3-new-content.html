Extreme Danger — Extension Springs

Extension springs under tension store lethal energy. Unlike torsion springs (contained on a shaft), a broken extension spring becomes a high-velocity projectile if no safety cable is installed. Safety cables running through the spring center are mandatory — they capture the spring fragments if it breaks. Never stand in line with an extension spring under tension.

Linear Force: F = kx

Extension springs follow Hooke's Law linearly: Force = spring constant (k) times extension distance (x). This produces a force curve that increases linearly with door travel. Compare with torsion springs where torque = spring constant times angular displacement. The linear characteristic creates an inherent mismatch with the door's non-linear weight curve at partial-open positions.

Pulley & Cable Routing

The cable routes from the bottom bracket of the door, up through a pulley at the horizontal track junction, then back to the spring's movable end. This 2:1 pulley arrangement halves the required spring force but doubles the travel distance. As the door opens, the spring stretches; as it closes, the spring contracts and stores energy for the next opening cycle.

Why Torsion Replaced Them

Torsion springs won for three reasons: (1) More uniform counterbalance across door travel due to cable drum geometry, (2) Safer failure mode — a broken torsion spring unwinds on its shaft rather than launching as a projectile, (3) Higher cycle life — torsion wire experiences less stress concentration than extension spring hooks. Extension springs remain on older and budget installations.

Safety Cable System

A steel cable threads through the hollow center of the extension spring coil, anchored at both ends to the track support structure. If the spring breaks, the cable contains the broken halves, preventing them from becoming projectiles. This simple $5 safety device prevents the most catastrophic extension spring failure mode. Many older installations lack safety cables — a critical retrofit item.

How It Works

The motor mounts on the wall beside the torsion spring shaft, driving it directly through a gear reduction unit. The motor's worm gear meshes with a spur gear on the torsion shaft, rotating the shaft to wind/unwind the springs and move the door via the existing cable drum system. No ceiling rail, no trolley, no chain or belt needed.

No Ceiling Rail

By driving the torsion shaft directly, jackshaft openers eliminate the entire ceiling-mounted rail assembly. This frees up overhead space for storage, high-lift track configurations, or cathedral ceilings where a traditional rail cannot be mounted. The entire mechanism lives at the wall above the door opening.

Use Cases

High ceilings: No rail means no ceiling clearance issues. Storage: Full ceiling available for overhead racks. Multi-door: One motor can potentially drive two single-car doors via a shared shaft. Best known model: LiftMaster 8500W (Wi-Fi enabled wall-mount).

Pros & Cons

Pros: Space savings, quiet DC motor, battery backup capable, no rail vibration, clean installation, works with high-lift tracks.

Cons: Higher cost ($350-$500+), requires torsion spring system (not compatible with extension springs), limited to doors under 180 sq ft typically.

AC Induction Motor

Traditional garage door motors use AC induction: a rotating magnetic field from stator windings induces current in the squirrel-cage rotor, creating torque. Speed is fixed by line frequency (60 Hz = 1725 RPM for 4-pole). Simple, rugged, cheap — but no speed control, harsh start/stop, and cannot run from a DC battery without an expensive inverter.

DC Permanent Magnet Motor

Modern openers use DC motors with permanent magnets on the stator and wound armature. Speed is controlled by varying input voltage via PWM (Pulse Width Modulation). This enables soft start (ramp up over 1-2 seconds), soft stop (decelerate before limits), and variable speed throughout travel. Runs directly from a 12V/24V battery.

Battery Backup System

A sealed lead-acid or lithium battery (typically 12V, 5-7Ah) provides 20-50 open/close cycles during power outages. An automatic transfer switch detects power loss and seamlessly switches to battery. The DC motor runs at reduced speed on battery to conserve energy. The battery charges continuously when AC power is available via a trickle charger circuit.

Variable Speed Benefits

DC PWM control enables: (1) Slow start reduces mechanical shock on door hardware, (2) Full speed mid-travel for fast operation, (3) Slow approach to limits reduces impact noise and extends limit switch life, (4) Adjustable overall speed via settings. The speed profile is programmable — S-curve acceleration is common in premium models.

Pro Tip — Battery Maintenance

Backup batteries have a 3-5 year lifespan regardless of use. The opener typically has a battery status LED. When the battery fails, the opener still works on AC power but loses backup capability. Replace proactively — a dead battery during a power outage means manually lifting a potentially 200+ lb door. Most use standard 12V 5Ah SLA batteries available at any battery retailer.

Systematic Approach

Professional technicians use a systematic approach: identify the symptom, isolate the subsystem (mechanical, electrical, electronic), test the simplest/cheapest cause first, then work toward complex causes. Most garage door problems fall into 5 categories: springs, tracks/rollers, opener/motor, sensors, and remotes/controls.

Most Common Issues

80% of service calls involve: broken springs (door won't open or feels very heavy), misaligned safety sensors (door reverses immediately), dead remote batteries, stripped gear in opener (motor runs but door doesn't move), and off-track rollers (door binds or jams). Each has a distinct diagnostic signature.

When to Call a Pro

DIY-safe: remote battery replacement, sensor alignment, lubrication, limit switch adjustment. Call a professional for: spring replacement (lethal tension), cable replacement (under spring tension), off-track doors (stored energy risk), unusual noises from the spring system, and any repair requiring winding bars.

UL 325 Standard

UL 325 (Underwriters Laboratories) mandates two independent entrapment protection systems for all residential garage door openers sold in the US since 1993. Both systems must function for the opener to operate in automatic close mode. Failure of either system must prevent automatic closing — the door can only be closed by holding the wall button with line-of-sight to the door.

Photoelectric Sensors

Two sensors mounted 6" above the floor on each side of the door opening. The sending unit emits a modulated infrared beam (not a simple LED — it's pulsed at a specific frequency to reject ambient light interference). The receiving unit detects this modulated signal. If the beam is broken during closing, the opener immediately reverses. LED indicators show alignment status.

Mechanical Force Sensing

The opener monitors motor current draw during closing. If the door contacts an obstruction, resistance increases, motor current spikes above the force threshold (adjustable via the "close force" knob). When current exceeds the threshold (equivalent to approximately 15 lb-ft at the door bottom edge), the motor reverses. This is the backup if photoelectric sensors fail.

Manual Release (Red Cord)

The red emergency release handle disconnects the trolley from the drive carriage via a spring-loaded lever. Pulling down and back on the cord compresses a spring latch, disengaging the trolley. The door can then be operated manually. Re-engagement is automatic: run the motor, and the carriage reconnects with the trolley. Essential during power outages and critical for fire egress.

Never Disable Safety Sensors

Bypassing photoelectric sensors is illegal and potentially lethal. A closing garage door exerts 200-400 lbs of force — enough to crush a child or pet. If sensors are misaligned (blinking LED), realign them rather than disconnecting. If sensor wires are damaged, replace them. The 30 seconds of inconvenience from a tripping sensor is infinitely preferable to the alternative.

Commercial

Hoist Operators

Heavy-duty operators for commercial sectional doors. Mount at the wall (like residential jackshaft) but with significantly more torque — 1-5 HP motors with industrial gear reduction. Often include:

  • 3-phase motor option for larger doors
  • Chain hoist manual backup
  • Adjustable speed via VFD
  • Industrial limit switches
Commercial

Rolling Steel Doors

No panels or tracks — the door is a curtain of interlocking steel slats that rolls up onto a barrel above the opening. Ideal for maximum headroom clearance and security.

  • Self-storing: no ceiling track needed
  • Wind-load rated models available
  • Security grilles for retail
  • Counter-balance via barrel spring
Commercial

Fire-Rated Doors

Required at openings between a garage and occupied building space, or between fire-separated areas. Must close automatically upon fire alarm activation.

  • UL labeled (1-4 hour ratings)
  • Fusible link auto-close mechanism
  • Connected to building fire alarm
  • Annual inspection required
Commercial

High-Cycle Springs

Commercial springs use premium oil-tempered wire (ASTM A229 Grade II) wound to tighter tolerances. Rated 50,000 to 100,000+ cycles vs. residential 10,000.

  • Larger wire diameter reduces stress %
  • Shot-peened for fatigue resistance
  • Often use dual spring on each side
  • 10-20 year lifespan at high usage
Commercial

3-Phase Motors

Large commercial doors use 3-phase power (208V/480V) for higher efficiency and smoother operation. Three overlapping AC sine waves produce a naturally rotating magnetic field — no start capacitor needed.

  • Higher power-to-weight ratio
  • Self-starting (no capacitor)
  • Longer motor lifespan
  • VFD compatible for speed control
Commercial

Control Systems

Commercial door operators integrate with building management systems (BMS) and offer advanced control options beyond simple remote/button activation.

  • Timer-based auto-close
  • Card/key fob access control
  • BMS integration (BACnet/Modbus)
  • Vehicle detection loops
